Batch Printing

How we reduced the time to print 5+ product tags from 28s to 12s — a 52% decrease.

Overview

Foodcamp is a web application that helps restaurant kitchens manage product expiration dates through automated tag printing. Before this feature, users could only print one tag at a time — a friction point that accumulated fast in a high-paced kitchen environment.

My role: UX designer and researcher, working alongside one designer, a UI designer, the CTO, CPO, and development team.

The Problem

Printing 5 or more tags meant repeating the same flow 5+ times. For kitchen staff working under time pressure, this wasn't just inconvenient — it was a daily bottleneck.

Discovery

We structured discovery in two phases: business and product.

Business phase

We ran benchmarking and stakeholder interviews with the CEO, operations, and commercial teams. The goal was clear: reduce printing time. But no one had a concrete direction for how.

Product phase

That answer came from users. We visited restaurants and interviewed kitchen staff directly, observing their environment, their pace, and their words. Two things happened during those sessions that shaped everything.

Users instinctively tried to multi-select products by long-pressing — the same way they'd select photos on a phone. That pattern confirmed the mental model, but it would have been costly to implement.

"You already group products into Groups. Why can't I just print the whole group at once?"

— Kitchen staff, field interview

That was our answer.

Solution

Foodcamp already had a product grouping structure. Batch Printing let users print an entire group in a single action, fitting naturally into an existing mental model rather than introducing a new one.

We moved quickly from lo-fi sketches to a high-fidelity prototype, ran usability tests with kitchen staff, and iterated based on two friction points they flagged: the Batch Print button was hard to find, and adding extra tag information wasn't intuitive. Given our timeline, we made a deliberate call to ship and monitor rather than delay.

The feature was built for tablets — the primary device used in kitchen environments.

Results

52%

Reduction in time to print 5+ tags — measured post-launch via Hotjar and Google Analytics

After launch, we measured behavior through Hotjar and Google Analytics. The 52% time reduction held. However, adoption was lower than expected. Most users continued using the single-tag path.

The diagnosis: the new path wasn't visible enough. The feature existed, but discovery was a problem. An improvement was identified but not prioritized at the time.

What I'd Do Differently

Usability testing validated the concept, but not the findability. I'd push for at least one additional test focused specifically on navigation and discoverability before shipping, or design an onboarding trigger to surface the feature to existing users post-launch.
